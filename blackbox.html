Black Box Investigation

Materials Used

  • Funnel
  • Water
  • Cardboard
  • Tubes

Project Overview

In New Media and Tech, we were challenged with predicting what is inside the Mystery Black Box. The black box is made out of black plastic that is locked so we can't see what is inside. Instead, we had to make careful observations and design experiments to figure out how it works.

Our task was to build physical models to test whether our predictions could match the behavior of the mystery black box. We used funnels, tubes, and water containers to experiment with water flow, measuring how much was poured in. By testing different designs, we explored how water might move through hidden tubes inside the box.

We also recreated our models in Autodesk Maya, building 3D versions of our black box with tubes and funnels to animate and visualize the water flow. This digital modeling helped us better understand and communicate whether our predictions about the mystery box could be accurate.

CER (Claim, Evidence, Reasoning)

Claim: The mystery black box is not exactly like the one we made, but our model demonstrates a similar concept: it contains a system of hidden tubes that controls water flow, allowing only a small amount of water to come out even when more is poured in.

Evidence: When water was poured into the real mystery box, only a small amount of water came out. My model contains funnels, tubes, and water containers, showing similar behaviors. Pouring 1000 mL of water resulted in only about 200 mL or less. Changing tube lengths and angles in our model consistently slowed the water and limited output, showing that different tubes could manage water flow, even if our model isn't the same as the original box.

Reasoning Part 1: Models are simplified versions of real systems that help us understand, test, and predict how something works. They are useful because we can experiment with them safely, observe patterns, and make predictions without opening or altering the real object. Models are usually simpler than what they represent, so they don't include every tiny detail, but they show the most important parts or behaviors.

Reasoning Part 2: We should believe our claim because the behavior of water in our model matches the behavior of the real mystery black box. In both cases, pouring a large amount of water results in only a small amount coming out. The repeated measurements in our model, like pouring 1000 mL and getting a result of around 300 mL, support the idea that the hidden tubes control water flow.

Reasoning Part 3: An alternative claim would be that the box has no internal structure and water flows freely. If this were true, pouring more water would result in more water coming out proportional to what was poured in. In our test, it did not happen. Only a small portion of water came out, regardless of how much we poured in. Because the outcome predicted by the alternative claim was not observed, this supports my actual claim.
